All-Star Cheerleading

All-Star Cheerleading is cheerleading where you are not physically “cheering” a “main sport” on, but rather is the main sport itself. There are levels 1 to 7. Each team performs a 2 minute and 30 seconds routine composed of four major elements. The elements are stunting, tumbling, jumps and dance. Stunting is where the bases lift their flyers in a variety of creative positions that make up a partner stunt and includes formation of pyramids and baskets (where you physically throw your flyer in the air, she completes a skill, and you catch her). Tumbling is the flipping aspect of cheerleading with a variety of different skills such as somersaults, cart-wheels, back hand springs and other gymanastics skills. There are running starts, where you perform a sequence of moves across the floor or there is standing tumbling where you perform a shorter tumbling pass from a stationary position. Jumps are simply a combination of different jumps performed by the team and are all synchronized together. Finally, dance is the final component of your routine considered the “celebration” at the end of your routine, where you perform a wide range of choreographed motions. There are three main positions for team members. The roles are flyers, bases and thirds and I have been a third throughout my cheerleading career. A flyer is the person who is thrown in the air and performs moves at the top of the pyramid. A base is the person on either side of the stunt group holding the flyers by their feet. A third is the person on the back of the stunt group holding the flyers ankles for stability and power. In All-Star cheerleading, teams develop and learn a routine through the season and use the same routine in competetions throughout the year. As the season progresses, the routine gets upgraded to include more advanced moves and skills. All of my years competing in All-Star Cheerleading have been with the Cheersport Sport Sharks facility in Cambridge, Ontario.

Varsity Cheerleading

Varsity cheerleading is something that is more new to me, as this is my first year. I have joined Brock’s cheerleading team but due to Covid 19, this has all been online thus far. There are a lot of the same or similar components, such as stunting, jumps and tumbling but with a different aspect - Cheers! There are two forms of varsity cheerleading. Gameday cheering is when you are cheering for other people in a support role and you are not the main sport. In Brock’s case, the cheerleading team supports the basketball team. There is also a competitive form of varsity cheerleading where your team is the main attraction. Last season, the Brock team went to Florida to compete in the University World Cup Cheerleading Championships (UWCC). In varsity cheerleading, the actual routine is 1 minute and 45 seconds and set to music, and the remainder of the time is spent performing vocal cheers with signs and pompoms. Part of your score for varsity cheerleading is based on the level of the crowd's engagement with your performance which is another difference from All-Star cheerleading.
